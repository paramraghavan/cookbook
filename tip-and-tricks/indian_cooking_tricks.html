Use Black Salt (Kala Namak) as a Finisher

Finish salads, cucumber, tomato, chaat-style snacks, and even watermelon with a small pinch of black salt instead of ordinary table salt. Kala namak has sulfurous, savory compounds that read as more than saltiness: it adds an eggy, umami-like depth and makes simple raw foods taste layered. Use it at the end, not as the main cooking salt, because a little goes a long way.

Soak Onions in Ice Water Before Cutting

Put onions in ice water for about 10 minutes before chopping when you have a lot of prep to do. The cold water pulls out some of the water-soluble sulfur compounds and slows the enzyme reaction that creates tear-inducing vapors. Drain before cutting; the result is cleaner, less irritating onion prep.

Add a Pinch of Baking Soda to Lentils and Beans

For dried lentils or beans, add only a tiny amount of baking soda, about 1/4 teaspoon per cup of dry lentils or beans. The alkaline water weakens pectin in the cell walls, helping legumes soften faster and more evenly. Keep the amount small: too much baking soda can make dal taste soapy and flat.

Bloom Spices in Fat Before Adding Liquid

Cook whole or ground spices briefly in hot oil, butter, or ghee before adding water, tomatoes, stock, or yogurt. Many spice aromas dissolve and disperse better in fat than in water, and a short bloom wakes them up while softening harsh volatile notes. Even 30 seconds in hot fat can make the finished dish taste deeper and more coherent.

27

Use Starchy Vegetable Water in Indian Gravies

Save the water from boiling potatoes or vegetables and use it to adjust curry or gravy consistency instead of plain water. Like pasta water, it carries starch and some vegetable flavor, so it helps sauces cling, thicken lightly, and taste less diluted. This is especially useful after boiling potatoes for aloo dishes.

26

Massage Salt into Bitter Greens Before Cooking

Rub a generous pinch of salt into methi, spinach, kale, chard, collards, or other greens about 5 minutes before cooking. The salt draws out moisture through osmosis and begins taming bitterness, so the greens wilt faster, cook more evenly, and taste rounder. This is especially helpful for assertive or fibrous greens.

25

Keep Cut Eggplant in Salt Water

As you cut eggplant for baingan bharta, brinjal curry, or a stir-fry, drop the pieces into lightly salted water. This limits oxygen exposure, slowing browning, while also drawing out some bitterness. Pat the eggplant dry before cooking; the pieces will be a bit denser and will absorb less oil.

24

Use a Spoon Handle to Check Frying Oil

Dip the handle of a wooden spoon, or the back of a metal spoon, into the oil. Steady bubbles mean the oil is around 350°F and ready for most frying. Violent sizzling means it is too hot; no bubbles means it needs more time. This gives you a practical oil-temperature cue when you do not have a thermometer.

Add Whole Aromatics to Rice Water

Add a whole green cardamom pod to rice cooking water for a subtle floral sweetness that perfumes the grains without making the rice taste obviously spiced. A small cinnamon stick or a couple of cloves can do the same job. Remove the whole spices before serving, or leave them for diners to push aside.

22

Freeze Ginger and Grate It Directly

Store whole, unpeeled ginger in the freezer and grate it on a microplane while still frozen. The peel grates finely enough to disappear into the dish, and freezing breaks down cell walls enough to help the ginger release juice and aroma quickly in the pan. It saves peeling, chopping, and waste.

Use Yogurt as a Gentle Meat Tenderizer

Marinate chicken, lamb, or similar meats in yogurt mixed with spices for a few hours or overnight. Yogurt's lactic acid tenderizes more gently than vinegar or lemon juice alone, so the meat stays juicy rather than turning mushy. It also helps form a flavorful crust under high heat, as in tandoori-style cooking.

20

Finish Dishes with a Fresh Tadka

Make a final tempering by heating spices in a small pan of oil or ghee, then pour the sizzling fat over dal, soup, or some yogurt-based dishes just before serving. Because the aromatics are released at the very end, the dish smells vivid before the first bite. It is a fast way to add a fresh top-note to cooked food.

Rest Roti Dough Before Rolling

Let roti, naan, or paratha dough rest at least 30 minutes under a damp cloth. Resting gives flour time to hydrate and lets gluten relax, so the dough rolls smoothly instead of springing back or tearing. The same principle helps pizza dough, pie crust, and other flour-based doughs behave better.

Add Ghee or Oil to Rice Water

Add a little ghee, neutral oil, or butter to the water when cooking rice, especially long-grain basmati. The fat lightly coats surface starch, helping the grains stay fluffy and separate instead of clumping together. Ghee also gives the rice a quiet richness without turning it into a heavy dish.

Use Hing with Dal, Beans, and Legumes

Bloom a tiny pinch of hing, also called asafoetida, in hot fat when cooking lentils or beans. Raw hing smells intense, but in oil it becomes deeply savory, with an onion-garlic character. Traditional Indian cooking also uses it to make legumes feel easier to digest. Use a very small amount, around 1/8 teaspoon or less for a pot.

Score Vegetables Before Marinating

Make shallow crosshatch cuts on large mushrooms, zucchini, bell peppers, cauliflower florets, or paneer before marinating. The cuts increase surface area and give marinade somewhere to go, so the seasoning penetrates instead of sitting only on the outside. Rub the marinade into the cuts and rest at least 20 minutes.

Soak Basmati Rice, but Do Not Overdo It

Rinse basmati, soak it for about 20 to 30 minutes, then drain and cook. That short soak hydrates the grains so they cook more evenly and are less sticky on the surface. Long soaking makes the grains fragile and more likely to break. A standard starting ratio is about 1 part rice to 1 1/2 parts water.

Use Kashmiri Chili Powder for Color

Use Kashmiri chili powder when you want the deep red color of restaurant-style Indian curries and marinades without intense heat. It is much milder than many chili powders but very vivid, so it improves appearance while keeping the dish approachable. It can also stand in for paprika when you want more character.

Use Half an Onion to Monitor Frying Oil

Place half an onion, cut side down, into oil as it heats. When the cut face turns lightly golden and sizzles steadily, the oil is ready for frying. Remove the onion before adding the food. The onion also leaves a faint savory sweetness in the oil, which can improve the fried item.

Freeze Curry Base in Portions

Cook onion, tomato, ginger, and garlic down into a thick curry base in a large batch, then freeze it in ice cube trays or small containers. That base is the time-consuming foundation for many curries. With frozen portions ready, you can start a weeknight curry with real cooked-down flavor instead of rebuilding the base every time.

Clean Sticky Pans with a Cut Potato

Rub the cut side of a potato over a warm cast iron, stainless, or kadai-style pan to lift sticky starches, sugars, and caramelized residue. Potato starch acts as a gentle abrasive, and its mild acidity helps loosen cooked-on food. Rinse afterward. It is useful when you want cleaning power without harsh scraping.

Use Cold Butter for Flaky Parathas

For flakier parathas, work very cold butter into the flour quickly, then add just enough water to make the dough and let it rest. Small cold fat pieces create steam pockets on the hot griddle, which helps form layers. Grating frozen butter into the flour is the easiest way to distribute it without warming it too much.

Infuse Water with Coriander Seeds

Soak lightly crushed coriander seeds in cool water overnight to unlock their natural essential oils. The infusion is traditionally used as a morning digestive drink in an empty stomach, has a gentle citrusy, floral flavor and aids digestion, kindles your digestive fire, and helps prevent bloating. You can also use coriander water to cook rice or as the liquid in a light sauce for subtle aroma.

Use a Little Tamarind for Hidden Depth

Add a very small amount of tamarind concentrate when a dish needs depth but not obvious sourness. In restrained quantities, tamarind reads as fruity, savory complexity rather than sharp acidity. Try about 1/2 teaspoon in curries, lentils, meat gravies, pasta sauce, stew, or braises, then adjust carefully.

Dry Roast Whole Spices Before Grinding

Toast whole spices in a dry pan over medium heat for 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, then cool completely before grinding. Heat drives off moisture, unlocks aromas inside the seed coats, and creates more complex flavor. Freshly roasted cumin, coriander, cardamom, and similar spices are far more vivid than pre-ground jars.

Save Pressure-Cooked Chickpea Liquid

Do not discard the liquid from pressure-cooked dried chickpeas. It is rich in starch, protein, and chickpea flavor, often more concentrated than canned aquafaba. Use it to thin hummus, build soup, add body to curries, or even whip it into a foam. Treat it as a useful cooking liquid, not waste.

Make Instant Buttermilk with Lemon and Milk

Stir 1 tablespoon fresh lemon juice into 1 cup whole milk and let it sit for about 5 minutes. The acid curdles the milk proteins into a tangy, thickened liquid that works like buttermilk in batters, quick marinades, pakora-style mixtures, pancakes, biscuits, and cakes. It is a useful substitute when you need acidity quickly.

Stir Egg into Dal for Protein and Silkiness

After dal is fully cooked and tempered, crack in one or two eggs while the pot is gently simmering and stir immediately. The eggs form tiny tender curds that distribute through the dal, adding protein and a silky body different from cream or coconut milk. Season again and serve right away.

Toast Pre-Mixed Spice Blends Before Using

Briefly toast a small amount of garam masala, curry powder, or another spice blend in a dry pan for about 60 seconds before adding it to a dish. The heat revives stale aromas, drives off flat notes, and makes older spices taste more active. This is especially useful for jars that have been opened many times.

Cook Onions Until Dark, Sweet, and Jammy

For curry bases, do not stop when onions are merely pale or golden. Cook them over medium-low heat for 25 to 30 minutes until dark brown, soft, sweet, and jammy. The last stretch develops deep Maillard and caramelized flavors that cannot be rushed. High heat burns onions and makes them bitter; low and slow is the point.

Use Mustard Seeds as an Oil Thermometer

Add a few whole black or brown mustard seeds to cold oil, then heat the pan. When the seeds sputter, crackle, and pop, the oil is in the right range for adding aromatics and spices without scorching them. This simple cue helps you stop guessing, avoid burned garlic or ginger, and start tempering at the right moment.

Practical Speed and Cleanup Tips

These bonus tips are not from the source video. Duplicate ideas from the original 31 have been removed, so this section focuses on extra prep, storage, preservation, and cleanup shortcuts used in vegetarian kitchens.

Bonus 1

Grate Onions When You Need Fast Browning

For quick gravies, grate or food-process onions instead of slicing them. The smaller pieces shed moisture fast and cook into a base quickly. Use medium heat and stir often because grated onion moves from golden to burnt faster than sliced onion.

Bonus 2

Chop Cilantro and Freeze It in a Towel-Lined Box

Wash cilantro, dry it very well, chop stems and leaves, and store it with a paper towel in the fridge for several days. For longer storage, freeze chopped cilantro in a thin layer. Use frozen cilantro in cooked dishes and fresh cilantro for garnish.

Bonus 3

Freeze Green Chilies Whole

Wash and dry green chilies completely, remove stems if you like, and freeze them whole. They slice easily while frozen and go straight into tadka, chutney, or curry. This prevents the sad half-used chili bag from dying in the fridge.

Bonus 4

Microwave Potatoes Before Aloo Sabzi

For fast aloo dishes, put cubed potatoes in a microwave-safe bowl with 1 to 2 tablespoons of water, cover loosely, and microwave until just partly cooked. Do not submerge them; the small amount of water creates steam. Drain any excess water, then finish the potatoes in the spiced pan so they still pick up masala flavor.

Bonus 5

Use Frozen Peas Straight from the Freezer

Do not thaw peas for matar paneer, pulao, poha, upma, or quick sabzi. Add them frozen near the end. They heat in minutes, stay greener, and avoid the mushy texture that comes from thawing and cooking too long.

Bonus 6

Line the Counter Before Rolling Rotis

Before making rotis or parathas, put down a large tray, silicone mat, or parchment sheet under the rolling area. Flour stays contained, cleanup is faster, and you can lift the whole mess away instead of wiping a wide dusty counter.

Bonus 7

Use Baking Soda Paste on Turmeric Stains

For turmeric-stained counters, cutting boards, or utensils, rub with a paste of baking soda and water, let it sit, then wash. Sunlight also fades turmeric stains naturally, especially on plastic containers and boards.

Bonus 8

Peel Garlic by Shaking It

For a lot of garlic, separate cloves, put them in a lidded container or between two bowls, and shake hard for 20 to 30 seconds. Many skins loosen on their own. It is much faster than peeling each clove with a knife.

Bonus 9

Microwave Lemons and Limes Before Juicing

Microwave citrus for 10 to 15 seconds, then roll it firmly on the counter before cutting. You get more juice with less squeezing, which is useful when finishing chaat, dal, chutneys, raita, or grilled foods.

Bonus 10

Batch-Cook and Freeze Plain Dal

Pressure-cook plain lentils with turmeric and freeze in portions before adding final tadka. Later, thaw a portion and finish it differently each time: cumin-garlic, curry leaf, tomato, spinach, or lemon-cilantro.

Bonus 11

Store Curry Leaves for Longer

Wash curry leaves only if needed, dry them completely, and store them wrapped in a paper towel in an airtight box. For longer storage, freeze the leaves. They will darken but still work well in hot oil for tempering.

Bonus 12

Make Chutney Concentrate

Blend cilantro-mint chutney thick, with minimal water, and freeze it in small cubes. Thin a cube later with yogurt, water, lemon juice, or coconut depending on the meal. Concentrate takes less freezer space and tastes fresher than watery frozen chutney.

Bonus 13

Use a Bench Scraper for Fast Cleanup

A bench scraper clears chopped vegetables, flour, onion skins, and sticky dough faster than a knife or paper towel. It is especially useful after roti, paratha, dosa batter spills, or big onion prep.

Bonus 14

Keep Emergency Cooked Rice Frozen

Freeze cooked rice in flat single-meal portions. Reheat with a sprinkle of water, covered, until steamy. It is perfect for lemon rice, curd rice, fried rice, quick pulao, or as a base for leftover curry.

Bonus 15

Blanch Greens, Then Squeeze Them Dry

For spinach, methi, amaranth, or mustard greens, blanch briefly in salted boiling water, shock in cold water, then squeeze dry before chopping. Professional vegetarian kitchens do this for faster service, brighter color, less bitterness, and cleaner saag or palak texture.

Bonus 16

Par-Cook Hard Vegetables Before the Masala

Steam or microwave carrots, cauliflower, beans, beets, and potatoes until halfway tender before adding them to a finished masala. The vegetables cook evenly, the masala does not burn while waiting for them to soften, and mixed sabzi finishes much faster.

Bonus 17

Roast Vegetables on a Sheet Pan Before Curry

For cauliflower, mushrooms, bell peppers, carrots, and squash, roast first on a hot sheet pan, then fold into the curry base. This removes water, adds browning, and keeps the gravy from turning thin. It is a restaurant-style way to get deeper flavor without babysitting a pan.

Bonus 18

Salt Mushrooms Early and Cook Them Wide

For mushroom masala or pulao, use a wide pan and salt mushrooms early so they dump their water quickly. Cook until that water evaporates before adding heavy spices or sauce. This prevents rubbery mushrooms and watery gravy.

Bonus 19

Use the Two-Pan Method for Dry Sabzi

Cook the masala in one pan and the vegetable in another, then combine at the end. Professional cooks use this when vegetables release lots of water or need browning. It keeps the spice base concentrated and avoids mushy, over-stirred vegetables.

Bonus 20

Cool Cooked Curries in Shallow Containers

For storage, do not put a deep hot pot straight into the fridge. Divide dal, chana, rajma, sambar, or curry into shallow containers so it cools quickly, then refrigerate or freeze. This is safer and preserves texture better than slow cooling in one large vessel.

Bonus 21

Freeze Gravy and Solids Separately When Possible

For paneer, kofta, pakora kadhi, and delicate vegetable curries, freeze the gravy separately and add the fragile pieces fresh or after reheating. This prevents paneer from becoming rubbery, kofta from breaking, and fried items from turning soggy.

Bonus 22

Leave Dairy and Fresh Herbs Out Until Reheating

If you plan to store a curry, stop before adding cream, yogurt, coconut milk, cilantro, or lemon. Add those after reheating. The base keeps longer and reheats better, while the finishing ingredients taste fresh instead of split, dull, or overcooked.

Bonus 23

Store Fried Snacks on a Rack, Not Paper

For pakoras, vadas, and cutlets, drain on a wire rack set over a tray instead of stacking on paper towels. Air circulation keeps the crust crisper. If holding for later, cool fully before boxing so trapped steam does not soften everything.

Bonus 24

Re-Crisp Fried Items in an Oven or Air Fryer

To revive pakoras, samosas, cutlets, or vadas, reheat on a rack in a hot oven or air fryer instead of microwaving. The dry heat drives off surface moisture and restores crunch. Microwave only if softness is acceptable.

Bonus 25

Oil the Knife for Sticky Vegetables

For jackfruit, okra, plantain, and other sticky vegetables, lightly oil the knife and your hands before cutting. The sap or slime sticks less, prep goes faster, and cleanup is easier. Reapply a thin film of oil as needed.

Bonus 26

Clean Okra Dry, Then Cut It Dry

Wash okra ahead of time and dry it completely before cutting. Wet okra becomes slimier and makes both the board and knife harder to clean. Professional cooks wash early, air-dry on towels, then cut only when fully dry.

Bonus 27

Pre-Soak Chickpeas and Rajma, Then Freeze

Soak dried chickpeas or rajma overnight, drain them very well, and freeze the soaked beans in recipe-size portions. Later, cook them straight from frozen in the pressure cooker or Instant Pot. You get the benefit of overnight soaking without having to plan the final dish a day ahead.

# SAT English 750+ Plan — Math Brain Student

## The Core Mindset Shift

SAT English is **not a reading test**. It is a **logic test with words**.

| Current Mode (680)                          | Target Mode (750+)                                 |
|---------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| "What does this passage mean?"              | "What's wrong here — and what's the tightest fix?" |
| Reading every word, getting lost in content | Scanning for structural flaws like a code reviewer |
| Playing defense                             | Playing offense                                    |

A chess or game player already thinks in patterns and traps. That's exactly what this test rewards. The underlying
ability is already there — it just needs to be redirected.

---

## Strategy 1 — Order of Operations (Strategic Skipping)

### What it is

Most students open the SAT English section and work straight through from question 1 to the end. This is a mistake.
Questions are NOT arranged by difficulty — they are arranged by **category**. That means an easy grammar question might
be sitting at question 22, right after a hard reading comprehension question at question 21. If you spend 3 minutes
grinding question 21, you may never even reach question 22 — a question you could have answered in 15 seconds.

The fix: **treat the section like a chess opening**. Play the fast, high-percentage moves first to bank time, then use
that time on the slower, harder positions.

### How to do it — step by step

**Step 1:** When the section starts, scroll through the questions quickly and identify the question types. Look for:

- **Transition questions** — a blank in the middle of a sentence where you choose a connecting word (however, therefore,
  furthermore, etc.)
- **Standard English Conventions** — punctuation, grammar, sentence structure questions
- These typically appear from question 18 onward but can appear anywhere

**Step 2:** Do ALL transition and grammar questions first, in any order. These require little to no reading of the full
passage — just the sentence or two surrounding the blank.

**Step 3:** Return to the reading comprehension questions with the time you've saved.

### The three transition categories to master

Every transition question falls into one of three logical relationships. Learn to spot them instantly:

| Relationship     | Signal words in the sentence                            | Correct transition                         |
|------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| **Contrast**     | "but," "yet," "despite," "although," two opposing ideas | however, nevertheless, on the other hand   |
| **Addition**     | Two ideas that agree or build on each other             | furthermore, additionally, moreover, also  |
| **Cause/Effect** | One thing leads to another                              | therefore, thus, consequently, as a result |

### Example — Transition Question

> "The researcher found the results to be inconclusive _______ the data suggested a slight upward trend."
>
> - ~~A) therefore~~ — wrong: "therefore" signals cause/effect, not contrast
> - **B) however ✓** — correct: "inconclusive" vs "upward trend" are opposing ideas = contrast
> - ~~C) furthermore~~ — wrong: "furthermore" adds agreement, but the ideas disagree
> - ~~D) specifically~~ — wrong: not a logical connector at all

**The logic move — 3 steps, 15 seconds:**

1. Read the sentence. Identify the two key ideas: *"inconclusive"* and *"upward trend"*
2. Ask: do these two ideas agree, disagree, or cause each other? → They **disagree** (contrast)
3. Pick the contrast transition → **however**

No reading of the full passage needed. No context needed. Pure logic.

> **Why this matters for score:** Transition questions are among the fastest points on the entire test. A student who
> masters all three categories can solve approximately 20% of the section in under 5 minutes — leaving a full time buffer
> for the harder reading questions.

## Strategy 2 — Read Like an Editor, Not a Reader

### What it is

The single biggest reason high-math students underperform on SAT English is that they read **for meaning**. They want to
understand the passage. But the SAT does not reward understanding — it rewards **finding flaws**. Every wrong answer has
a specific, identifiable error. Every right answer is the tightest, most precise, most logically defensible option.

Think of it this way: a chess player doesn't ask "what is my opponent thinking?" — they ask "what is the weakest move on
the board?" That's the same shift needed here.

### How to build this skill — the editor drill

**Daily practice (10–15 minutes):** Take any short opinion piece (NYT, WSJ, The Atlantic editorial) and read it with
this checklist:

**While reading each sentence, ask:**

- Is any word vague or could it be more precise? (e.g., "thing," "stuff," "very," "really")
- Is any word redundant — does it repeat what was already said?
- Does this sentence connect logically to the one before it?

**After each paragraph, ask:**

- Does this paragraph belong here, or would it make more sense earlier or later?
- What is the ONE job this paragraph is doing? (introducing, supporting, contrasting, concluding)
- If I removed this paragraph, would the essay still make sense?

**The question to ask after every sentence:**
> "What is wrong with this, or what could be tighter, clearer, or more precise?"
> *(Not: "What does this mean?")*

### Why this maps directly to SAT question types

| SAT question type                                            | What the editor drill trains                |
|--------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| "Which choice most effectively combines the sentences?"      | Spotting redundancy and wordiness           |
| "Which transition best connects paragraph 2 to paragraph 3?" | Identifying logical flow between ideas      |
| "Where should this sentence be placed?"                      | Recognizing paragraph structure and purpose |
| "Which choice is most precise?"                              | Eliminating vague language                  |

### Example — Before and After Editor Thinking

**Original sentence in a passage:**
> "The new policy had a big and very significant impact on many people in the local area."

**Editor brain flags:**

- "big and very significant" = redundant (pick one)
- "many people in the local area" = vague (how many? which people? what area?)
- The sentence is doing the same job twice

**SAT question:** "Which choice most effectively improves the sentence?"
> - ~~A) "had a big impact on many local residents"~~ — still vague
> - **B) "reduced commute times for over 40,000 daily bus riders" ✓** — specific, precise, no redundancy
> - ~~C) "significantly impacted a large number of people"~~ — still redundant
> - ~~D) "was very impactful for the community"~~ — "very impactful" is weak

The editor brain eliminates A, C, and D automatically because they're imprecise or redundant. B wins.

## Strategy 3 — The Literalism Rule (Poison Pill Elimination)

### What it is

The most common way strong students lose points on SAT English is by choosing an answer that "seems right" or "could be
true." The SAT is not asking what *could* be true. It is asking what the passage *directly supports*. This is a critical
difference.

Think of each answer choice like a line of code. If even one variable is undefined — one word that isn't supported by
the text — the entire answer throws an error. It doesn't matter how right the rest of it is. **99% correct = 0% correct.
**

This is called the **poison pill**: one word deliberately inserted into an otherwise attractive answer to make it wrong.

### How to apply it — step by step

**Step 1:** After reading an answer choice, do not ask "does this sound right?" Ask: **"Can I find direct support in the
passage for every single claim in this answer?"**

**Step 2:** Go word by word through the answer choice. Find the specific sentence or phrase in the passage that
justifies each key word.

**Step 3:** If any word or phrase in the answer choice goes beyond what the passage actually says — even slightly —
eliminate it immediately.

**Step 4:** The correct answer will be almost boring in how directly it mirrors the passage. It usually feels "too
simple." That's the point.

### Example 1 — Single poison pill

**Passage says:** "The scientist was *surprised* by the results."

**Answer choices:**

- ~~A) "The scientist was shocked by the failure of the experiment."~~
- ~~B) "The scientist had not expected the experiment to succeed."~~
- **C) "The scientist did not anticipate the outcome." ✓**
- ~~D) "The scientist was disappointed that the results were inconclusive."~~

**Breaking down each choice:**

- **A:** "shocked" is stronger than "surprised" (not supported). "Failure" is never mentioned (poison pill). Eliminate.
- **B:** "had not expected it to succeed" implies the scientist expected failure — the passage only says they were
  surprised. Eliminate.
- **C:** "did not anticipate the outcome" = directly mirrors "surprised by the results." Every word is supported.
  Correct.
- **D:** "disappointed" and "inconclusive" are never mentioned anywhere. Two poison pills. Eliminate immediately.

### Example 2 — Subtle poison pill

**Passage says:** "The program was *popular* among students."

**Answer choice:** "Students *enthusiastically supported* the program."

**The poison pill:** "Enthusiastically supported" goes further than "popular." Something can be popular without active,
enthusiastic support (people might use it simply because it's convenient). The passage doesn't say students *actively
supported* it — only that it was popular. Eliminate.

> **The rule of thumb:** If the passage uses a mild word (popular, surprised, interested), any answer choice that
> escalates it to a stronger word (enthusiastically supported, shocked, passionate) is automatically suspect.

### Common poison pill patterns to watch for

| Passage word | Poison pill escalation              |
|--------------|-------------------------------------|
| surprised    | shocked, stunned, horrified         |
| popular      | beloved, enthusiastically supported |
| concerned    | alarmed, opposed, against           |
| improved     | solved, fixed, eliminated           |
| suggested    | proved, demonstrated, confirmed     |

## Strategy 4 — Search and Destroy (Question-First Reading)

### What it is

Reading the full passage before looking at the questions is one of the biggest time-wasters on the SAT. A typical SAT
passage is 500–750 words. If you read it fully before knowing what the questions ask, you are processing hundreds of
words of information you may never need.

The fix: **read the question first, then hunt for exactly the data it asks for.** Treat the passage like a database and
the question like a search query. You are not reading — you are retrieving.

### How to do it — step by step

**Step 1:** Read the question stem completely. Identify the *exact* thing being asked. Is it:

- A specific claim to support?
- A specific word or phrase to interpret?
- The main idea of a paragraph?
- The function of a particular sentence?

**Step 2:** Convert the question into a search target. Ask yourself: "If the answer to this question is in the passage,
what would it look like? What keywords or data types am I hunting for?"

**Step 3:** Skim the passage for those keywords or data types only. Do not read full sentences — just scan for the
signal.

**Step 4:** Once you find the relevant section, read only that section carefully. Then answer the question.

### Example 1 — Command of Evidence (Quantitative)

**Question:** "Which choice best supports the claim that the urban heat island effect is worsening?"

**Step 1:** The claim is "urban heat island effect is worsening." I need evidence that it's getting worse over time.

**Step 2:** Convert to a search target: I'm looking for numbers, data, or comparisons that show a trend over time.
Specifically: temperature measurements where the gap between city and rural temperatures is increasing.

**Step 3:** Scan for: numbers, years, temperatures, comparisons, words like "increased," "widened," "grown."

**Step 4:** Find the sentence: *"In 1990 the average city temperature exceeded rural areas by 2°F; by 2020 that gap had
grown to 5°F."* That's the answer.

**What to skip entirely:** The paragraph explaining what a "heat island" is, the paragraph about why cities get hot, the
author's introduction. None of that is your target data.

### Example 2 — Interpreting a word in context

**Question:** "As used in line 34, the word 'cultivated' most nearly means..."

**Step 1:** Find line 34 immediately. Read only that sentence and the one before and after it.

**Step 2:** Cover up the word "cultivated." What word would make sense in this exact context? (e.g., if the sentence is
about a politician building relationships over time, the meaning is closer to "developed" than "farmed.")

**Step 3:** Match your predicted word to the answer choices. Eliminate choices that fit the dictionary definition of "
cultivated" but not the contextual meaning.

> **Key principle:** SAT vocabulary questions are never about the most common definition of a word. They always test a
> secondary or contextual meaning. Always define the word yourself from context before looking at the choices.

### Question types and what to scan for

| Question type                                           | Your search target                                                               |
|---------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| "Which choice provides the best evidence for..."        | Numbers, quotes, specific facts                                                  |
| "The author uses [phrase] mainly to..."                 | The sentence before and after the phrase                                         |
| "Which best describes the central idea of paragraph 3?" | First and last sentence of that paragraph                                        |
| "The word X most nearly means..."                       | The sentence containing X — ignore all other definitions                         |
| "Where should this sentence be added?"                  | Identify the topic of each paragraph, find where the new sentence logically fits |

## Strategy 5 — The 30-Second Cutoff (Time Management)

### What it is

A student with a math and chess background has been trained to solve every problem completely before moving on. In math,
this is exactly right — a half-solved problem is worth zero points. On SAT English, this instinct becomes a trap.

The reality of SAT English scoring: **every question is worth the same number of points.** A hard question you spend 3
minutes on is worth exactly the same as an easy question you answer in 15 seconds. Spending 3 minutes on one hard
question while 5 easy questions wait is mathematically losing.

### The chess clock analogy

In blitz chess, you don't spend 90% of your clock on the opening and leave 10 seconds for the endgame. You manage time
across the whole game. The same principle applies here. The goal is not to "solve" every question — it is to **maximize
total correct answers in the time available.**

### How to apply it — the full protocol

**While working through a question:**

1. Read the question and all answer choices once
2. If the answer is clear → select it and move on immediately (target: under 30 seconds)
3. If you can eliminate 2 choices but are stuck between 2 → go to the 30-second rule

**The 30-second rule — when stuck between two answers:**

1. Note the time. You have 30 seconds from this moment.
2. Re-read the relevant passage section once more with the two remaining choices in mind.
3. Ask: "Which choice has zero words that go beyond what the passage says?" (apply the poison pill rule)
4. If still unsure after 30 seconds → **pick the more conservative, more literal answer** (the one that makes a smaller,
   more direct claim)
5. Flag the question in the Bluebook app
6. Move on immediately — do not look back

**At the end of the section:**

- Return to flagged questions only if time remains
- Never return to a question you already answered confidently

### Why the "more conservative answer" rule works

When genuinely unsure between two choices, the SAT almost always makes one answer slightly more extreme or slightly less
directly supported. The correct answer tends to be the more modest, more literal, more directly-evidenced one.
Defaulting to the conservative choice when guessing gives you better odds than random selection.

### Example — two remaining choices, time is up

**Two remaining choices:**

- A) "The author is *skeptical* of the new policy"
- B) "The author notes *concerns* about the new policy"

**The passage says:** "The policy may have unintended consequences."

**Apply the rule:** "May have unintended consequences" = concern, not full skepticism. "Skeptical" is a stronger claim
not fully supported. "Notes concerns" is the more conservative, more literal match.

**Pick B.** Flag. Move on.

> **The math:** If there are 54 questions and you spend an average of 90 seconds on each, you use the full time. If you
> spend 4 minutes on 3 hard questions, you're now averaging only 60 seconds on the remaining 51 — which means rushed
> answers and avoidable errors. The 30-second cutoff prevents this scenario entirely.

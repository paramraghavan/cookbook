# SAT English 750+ Plan 

## The Core Mindset Shift

SAT English is **not a reading test**. It is a **logic test with words**.

| Current Mode (680)                          | Target Mode (750+)                                 |
|---------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| "What does this passage mean?"              | "What's wrong here — and what's the tightest fix?" |
| Reading every word, getting lost in content | Scanning for structural flaws like a code reviewer |
| Playing defense                             | Playing offense                                    |

Like a chess/game player already thinks in patterns and traps. That's exactly what this test rewards. The underlying ability is
already there — it just needs to be redirected.

---

## Strategy 1 — Order of Operations (Strategic Skipping)

**Do grammar and transitions first.** Questions aren't arranged by difficulty — they're arranged by category. Skip the
long reading passages and attack Standard English Conventions and Transitions first (usually questions 18–20+). Bank
time for harder sections.

### Example — Transition Question

> "The researcher found the results to be inconclusive _______ the data suggested a slight upward trend."
>
> - ~~A) therefore~~
> - **B) however ✓**
> - ~~C) furthermore~~
> - ~~D) specifically~~

**The logic move:** Spot the conflict — "inconclusive" vs "upward trend" = contrast = *however*. This takes 15 seconds.
No reading needed.

> **Key insight:** Master contrast / addition / cause-effect transitions and you can solve ~20% of the test in under 5
> minutes.

---

## Strategy 2 — Read Like an Editor, Not a Reader

**Daily drill:** Read one opinion column (NYT, WSJ, The Atlantic) with a red pen. His job:

- Circle vague or redundant words
- Flag weak transitions
- Ask after each paragraph: "Does this belong here?"

**Ask this after every sentence:**
> "What's wrong with this — and what could be made tighter, clearer, or more precise?"
> *(Not: "What does this mean?")*

---

## Strategy 3 — The Literalism Rule (Poison Pill Elimination)

**99% right = 0% right.** One wrong word kills the answer choice. Treat it like a syntax error in code. Delete
immediately.

### Example

**Passage says:** "The scientist was *surprised* by the results."

**Answer choice (poison pill):** "The scientist was *shocked by the failure* of the experiment."

**The kill shot:** The word "failure" doesn't appear in the passage. The text said *surprised* — not shocked, and said
nothing about failure. Eliminate immediately. One bad word = wrong answer, full stop.

---

## Strategy 4 — Search and Destroy (Question-First Reading)

**Read the question before the passage.** Reading the full passage first wastes 30–45 seconds. Identify the "target
data" from the question stem, then scan the passage like a search query.

### Example — Command of Evidence Question

> "Which choice best supports the claim that the urban heat island effect is worsening?"

**The scan move:** Ignore the intro explaining what a heat island is. Search only for a mathematical relationship:
`[City Temp − Rural Temp] = increasing value`. Find the sentence with widening numbers. Done.

---

## Strategy 5 — The 30-Second Cutoff (Time Management)

**Flag, guess, move on.** Grinding one hard question while 5 easy ones wait is a losing endgame.

### The Protocol

If stuck for more than 30 seconds between two choices:

1. Pick your gut answer
2. Flag the question
3. Move on immediately
4. Return only if time remains at the end

> It's better to guess on one hard question and correctly answer five easy ones than to "solve" the hard one and leave
> three blank.

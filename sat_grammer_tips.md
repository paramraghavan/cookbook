# SAT Grammar Tips — Math Brain Student

## How to Think About SAT Grammar

SAT grammar is not about "feeling" whether a sentence sounds right. It is a **rule-based system** — exactly like a set
of mathematical axioms. Every question has one correct answer that can be proven using a specific rule. Learn the rules,
apply them mechanically, get the point.

There are roughly 8 grammar categories on the SAT. Master all 8 and grammar questions become automatic — no thinking
required, just pattern recognition.

---

## Category 1 — Punctuation (Semicolons, Colons, Commas, Dashes)

This is the highest-yield grammar category. Learn these 4 rules and you will answer most punctuation questions in under
20 seconds.

### The Independent Clause Test

Before applying any punctuation rule, you must know what an **independent clause** is:

> An independent clause = a complete sentence. It has a subject + a verb + a complete thought.
> - "The experiment failed." ✓ independent clause
> - "Because the experiment failed." ✗ NOT independent clause (incomplete thought)
> - "The data, which was collected over five years." ✗ NOT independent clause (no main verb)

---

### Rule 1 — Semicolons

**Formula:** `Independent clause ; Independent clause`

A semicolon connects two independent clauses. Both sides must be able to stand alone as complete sentences. Nothing
else.

**Correct:** "The experiment failed; the team had to start over."
**Incorrect:** "The experiment failed; causing the team to start over." ← "causing the team to start over" is not an
independent clause

> **Quick test:** Replace the semicolon with a period. If both halves are complete sentences, the semicolon is correct.
> If either half falls apart, the semicolon is wrong.

---

### Rule 2 — Colons

**Formula:** `Independent clause : [explanation, list, or elaboration]`

A colon introduces something that explains or expands on what came before. The left side of the colon must always be an
independent clause. The right side can be anything.

**Correct:** "The results were clear: the new drug reduced symptoms by 40%."
**Correct:** "She brought three things: a notebook, a pen, and a ruler."
**Incorrect:** "The results included: a 40% reduction in symptoms." ← "The results included" is not a complete sentence
on its own

---

### Rule 3 — Commas

Commas have four main jobs on the SAT:

| Job                                | Rule                                                                                  | Example                                                            |
|------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Join two independent clauses**   | Comma + coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so)           | "The data was clear, but the team disagreed."                      |
| **Set off an introductory phrase** | Comma after any phrase that opens the sentence before the subject                     | "After five years of research, the team published their findings." |
| **Set off a non-essential clause** | Commas around information that could be removed without changing the sentence meaning | "The researcher, who had worked there for a decade, retired."      |
| **Separate items in a list**       | Comma between each item                                                               | "The study examined age, diet, and exercise."                      |

**The comma splice error** (most common wrong answer on SAT):

> "The experiment failed, the team had to start over." ✗

Two independent clauses cannot be joined by a comma alone. Fix options:

- Add FANBOYS: "The experiment failed, so the team had to start over." ✓
- Use a semicolon: "The experiment failed; the team had to start over." ✓
- Make one clause dependent: "Because the experiment failed, the team had to start over." ✓

---

### Rule 4 — Dashes

**Single dash** = introduces an explanation or emphasis (same job as a colon, more dramatic)
> "There was one problem — the data was missing."

**Paired dashes** = set off a non-essential interruption (same job as paired commas, stronger emphasis)
> "The experiment — which had taken three years — finally produced results."

> **Key rule for paired dashes:** If you open with a dash, you must close with a dash. You cannot mix a dash with a
> comma: "The experiment — which had taken three years, finally produced results." ✗

## Category 2 — Subject-Verb Agreement

The verb must match the subject in number (singular/plural). Simple in theory — the SAT makes it hard by hiding the
subject.

### The three traps SAT uses

**Trap 1 — Prepositional phrase between subject and verb**

> "The results of the experiment *was* surprising." ✗

The subject is "results" (plural), not "experiment." Cross out the prepositional phrase "of the experiment" mentally:
> "The results ___ surprising." → needs "were" ✓

**How to spot it:** If there is a prepositional phrase (of, in, at, by, with, from, etc.) between the subject and verb,
cross it out before checking agreement.

**Trap 2 — Inverted sentence order**

> "Across the street *stand* three old buildings." ✓

The subject "buildings" comes after the verb. Reorder the sentence to check: "Three old buildings stand across the
street." ✓

**Trap 3 — Collective nouns and indefinite pronouns**

These words look plural but take singular verbs:

| Always singular                    | Always plural            |
|------------------------------------|--------------------------|
| everyone, everyone, no one, nobody | both, few, many, several |
| each, either, neither              |                          |
| anyone, anybody, someone, somebody |                          |
| the number of                      | a number of              |

> "Each of the students *was* assigned a project." ✓ (not "were")
> "A number of students *were* absent." ✓ (not "was")

## Category 3 — Pronoun Agreement & Ambiguity

### Rule 1 — Pronoun must agree with its antecedent in number

> "Each student must submit *their* assignment." ✗ (technically — SAT may flag this)
> "Each student must submit *his or her* assignment." ✓
> Better: "All students must submit *their* assignments." ✓ (rewrite to avoid the issue)

### Rule 2 — Pronoun reference must be unambiguous

> "When Maria met Sofia, *she* was nervous." ✗ — who is "she"? Maria or Sofia?

The SAT will offer a fix that replaces the ambiguous pronoun with the actual name:
> "When Maria met Sofia, *Maria* was nervous." ✓

### Rule 3 — Pronoun case (who vs. whom)

- **Who** = subject (doing the action): "The scientist *who* ran the experiment..."
- **Whom** = object (receiving the action): "The scientist *whom* they hired..."

**Quick test:** Substitute "he" or "him."

- If "he" fits → use "who"
- If "him" fits → use "whom"

> "Who/whom do you trust?" → "You trust him." → use **whom** ✓

## Category 4 — Verb Tense Consistency

The tense of verbs in a passage must stay consistent unless there is a logical reason to shift.

### The basic rule

If a passage is written in past tense, all verbs should be past tense. Do not switch to present tense mid-paragraph
without reason.

**Incorrect:** "The scientist ran the experiment and *finds* unexpected results."
**Correct:** "The scientist ran the experiment and *found* unexpected results." ✓

### Logical tense shifts (allowed)

Some tense shifts are correct because the timing genuinely differs:

> "The data, which *was* collected in 2010, *is* still relevant today."

The collection happened in the past (was collected) but the relevance is present (is). This shift is logical and
correct.

### The four tenses most tested on the SAT

| Tense           | Structure | Use                                            |
|-----------------|-----------|------------------------------------------------|
| Simple past     | "ran"     | Completed action in the past                   |
| Past perfect    | "had run" | Action completed before another past action    |
| Simple present  | "runs"    | Current facts, habits, general truths          |
| Present perfect | "has run" | Action started in the past, still relevant now |

**Common error — past perfect vs. simple past:**
> "By the time the results arrived, the team *left*." ✗
> "By the time the results arrived, the team *had left*." ✓

"Had left" = past perfect, showing the team's departure happened before the results arrived. Use past perfect whenever
one past action precedes another.

## Category 5 — Modifiers (Misplaced and Dangling)

A modifier is a word or phrase that describes something. It must be placed directly next to the thing it describes. When
it's not, you get a misplaced or dangling modifier — one of the SAT's favorite traps.

### Dangling modifier (most common)

A dangling modifier occurs when the opening descriptive phrase does not logically connect to the subject of the main
clause.

**Incorrect:** "Running down the street, the rain started to fall."

- Who is running? "The rain" — that makes no sense. The rain can't run.
- The subject of the main clause must be the one doing the running.

**Correct:** "Running down the street, *she* felt the rain start to fall." ✓

- Now "she" is running — logical.

### The SAT formula for dangling modifier questions

Almost every dangling modifier question looks like this:
> "[Opening participial phrase], [subject] [verb]..."

**The rule:** Whatever comes immediately after the comma must be the thing performing the action in the opening phrase.

**More examples:**

| Incorrect                                                  | Correct                                                            |
|------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|
| "Exhausted from the journey, the hotel room was a relief." | "Exhausted from the journey, *she* found the hotel room a relief." |
| "Having studied all night, the exam seemed easy."          | "Having studied all night, *he* found the exam easy."              |
| "To improve the results, a new method was developed."      | "To improve the results, *the team* developed a new method."       |

### Quick test for any opening phrase question

1. Identify the opening phrase (everything before the first comma)
2. Ask: what noun is performing the action in that phrase?
3. Check: is that noun the subject of the main clause (immediately after the comma)?
4. If not → the answer choice with the dangling modifier is wrong

## Category 6 — Parallelism

Items in a list or comparison must use the same grammatical form. This is called parallel structure.

### The basic rule

If you start a list with a verb ending in -ing, all items must end in -ing. If you start with a noun, all items must be
nouns. The form must be consistent.

**Incorrect:** "She enjoys reading, to write, and the study of grammar."

- "reading" = gerund (-ing)
- "to write" = infinitive
- "the study of" = noun phrase
  All three forms are different — not parallel.

**Correct:** "She enjoys *reading*, *writing*, and *studying* grammar." ✓
All three are gerunds (-ing).

### Parallelism in comparisons

**Incorrect:** "Running a marathon is more difficult than to complete a triathlon."
**Correct:** "Running a marathon is more difficult than *completing* a triathlon." ✓

**Incorrect:** "The new policy is faster, cheaper, and it is more efficient."
**Correct:** "The new policy is faster, cheaper, and *more efficient*." ✓

### SAT tip — scan for lists and comparisons

Whenever you see a list (X, Y, and Z) or a comparison (more X than Y), immediately check every item for parallel form.
This is where the SAT hides most parallelism errors.

## Category 7 — Conciseness and Redundancy

The SAT rewards tight, precise writing. Any answer choice that uses more words than necessary to express an idea is
wrong. Look for these patterns:

### Types of redundancy to eliminate

**Redundant pairs** — when two words say the same thing:

- "advance planning" → just "planning" (planning is always in advance)
- "past history" → just "history" (history is always past)
- "future plans" → just "plans"
- "end result" → just "result"
- "completely finished" → just "finished"
- "whether or not" → usually just "whether"

**Restating the obvious:**
> "The period of time during which the experiment was conducted lasted three years."
> Better: "The experiment lasted three years." ✓

**Wordy phrases with simple replacements:**

| Wordy                  | Concise |
|------------------------|---------|
| due to the fact that   | because |
| in the event that      | if      |
| at this point in time  | now     |
| is able to             | can     |
| has the ability to     | can     |
| in order to            | to      |
| the reason why is that | because |

### The SAT conciseness rule

When two answer choices are both grammatically correct and both convey the same meaning, **the shorter one is always
correct.** The SAT never rewards unnecessary words.

## Category 8 — Sentence Boundaries (Fragments and Run-ons)

### Fragments

A fragment is an incomplete sentence — it is missing a subject, a verb, or a complete thought.

**Common fragment types on the SAT:**

| Fragment type            | Example                                | Fix                                                                  |
|--------------------------|----------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Missing main verb        | "The data collected over five years."  | "The data *was* collected over five years."                          |
| Dependent clause alone   | "Although the results were promising." | "Although the results were promising, *the team continued testing*." |
| Participial phrase alone | "Running the experiment three times."  | "Running the experiment three times, *she confirmed the result*."    |

### Run-ons

A run-on occurs when two independent clauses are joined without proper punctuation or a conjunction.

**Incorrect:** "The experiment succeeded the team celebrated."
**Fix options:**

- Period: "The experiment succeeded. The team celebrated." ✓
- Semicolon: "The experiment succeeded; the team celebrated." ✓
- Comma + conjunction: "The experiment succeeded, and the team celebrated." ✓
- Subordinate one clause: "When the experiment succeeded, the team celebrated." ✓

## Quick Reference — Most Common SAT Grammar Errors

| Error type                | What to look for                              | Quick fix rule                                        |
|---------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Comma splice              | Two full sentences joined by only a comma     | Add FANBOYS or change to semicolon                    |
| Subject-verb disagreement | Prepositional phrase between subject and verb | Cross out the middle phrase, check subject alone      |
| Dangling modifier         | Opening phrase doesn't match the subject      | The noun after the comma must do the action           |
| Pronoun ambiguity         | "they," "it," "she" could refer to two things | Replace pronoun with the actual noun                  |
| Parallel structure        | List items in different grammatical forms     | Make all items the same form (-ing, noun, infinitive) |
| Redundancy                | Two answer choices mean the same thing        | Pick the shorter, more direct one                     |
| Wrong tense               | Tense shifts without logical reason           | Match the tense of surrounding sentences              |
| Semicolon misuse          | Non-independent clause after semicolon        | Both sides must be complete sentences                 |

## The Goal

Grammar questions should eventually require zero conscious thought. Like a chess player who has memorized opening
theory, the patterns should be instant. When you see `Independent clause , independent clause` — you immediately know
that needs a semicolon or a conjunction. When you see an opening participial phrase — you immediately check what noun
follows the comma.

**Target:** 95%+ accuracy on grammar questions, under 20 seconds each, freeing maximum time for the harder reading
comprehension questions.
